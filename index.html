🔍
Ethical failure is not an awareness problem
The primary driver is the suppression of ethical agency by fear and hierarchy — not ignorance of standards.
🔗
Stress and ethics are mutually reinforcing
Employee stress is partly generated by HR decisions made reluctantly under hierarchical pressure — a self-reinforcing cycle.

Organizations must address the agency deficit — making it structurally safe to act ethically — not just the awareness deficit.

⚖️
Structural & Workload Interventions
Evidence-based workload limits, workload audits, transparent overtime compensation, and performance targets calibrated to realistic capacity.
🛡️
Ethical Leadership & Psychological Safety
Independent reporting channels, whistleblower protections, and leaders who visibly model ethical behavior even at personal cost.
🧠
Emotional Intelligence & Values-based Development
Training that shifts from compliance to capability how to act on ethical convictions under pressure.
🌱
Employee Well being as Ethical Imperative
Mental health support and open communication treated as structural conditions for ethical agency not welfare add ons.

Case Study

Research as a Consulting Brief

Research Challenge · HRM · Indian Knowledge Systems
Why do ethical professionals act unethically even when they know better?
A mixed-method investigation into the knowing-doing divide in HRM, using the Mahabharata as a diagnostic lens and survey data from 120 respondents across Indian IT and services organizations.
Challenge
The Knowing-Doing Gap
HRM literature addresses ethical knowledge and context separately — leaving unexplained why people with strong ethical awareness routinely fail to act under organizational pressure.
Framework
IKS Theoretical Model
Applied Duryodhana's confession from the Mahabharata as a theoretical anchor. Built a three-part model: awareness → impedance → dissonance.
Methodology
Mixed-Method Design
Two structured surveys targeting different populations. Supplemented with secondary sources: peer-reviewed journals, corporate governance reports, and translated Mahabharata editions.
Data Collection
N = 120, Dual Surveys
Survey 1 (N=75 employees): workload, stress, overtime. Survey 2 (N=45 HR professionals): ethical decision-making and self-reported compromise. Anonymous responses to reduce bias.
Findings
Two Converging, Mutually Reinforcing Patterns
85% of employees attributed stress to workload intensity. 75% of HR professionals admitted acting against their ethical judgment under institutional pressure. These findings are causally linked: employee stress is partly a product of HR decisions made reluctantly under hierarchical directives. The "Duryodhana condition" — knowing what is right but lacking structural freedom to act on it — manifests at both individual and organizational levels.
Outcome & Impact
A New Lens for Organizational Ethics
Four actionable HRM recommendation clusters and a novel theoretical contribution: repositioning ethics from a knowledge problem (solved by training) to an agency problem (solved by restructuring conditions that suppress ethical action). Selected for national conference presentation and accepted for publication in proceedings.
